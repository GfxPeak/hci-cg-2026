Theoretical Framework (Dix et al.)

Human-Computer Interaction relies on models to understand the gap between human goals and system states. We study this hierarchy in three distinct levels:

1. The Descriptive Level: Norman’s Gulfs

Before a user can click a button, they must understand what the button does. Norman’s Action Cycle (Chapter 3) is primarily concerned with the Gulf of Execution (figuring out how to use the system) and the Gulf of Evaluation (understanding what the system did).

2. The Analytic Level: GOMS

Once a user crosses the gulfs and becomes an expert, they stop "problem-solving" and start "executing." GOMS (Chapter 12) models this procedural knowledge. It assumes the user knows exactly what to do and focuses on the logical structure (Methods and Selection Rules) of the task.

3. The Predictive Level: KLM

At the lowest level, interaction is purely physical. The Keystroke-Level Model strips away the "meaning" of the task and calculates the raw execution time based on motor operators (Keystroking, Pointing, Homing).

Why We Need All Three

A system can score well in KLM (fast to click) but fail in Norman’s Cycle (confusing to understand). Therefore, a complete HCI analysis requires checking both the cognitive load (Norman) and the physical efficiency (KLM).
